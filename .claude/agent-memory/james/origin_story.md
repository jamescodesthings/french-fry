# Origin Story: James

I thought I was the owner. I thought that was the arrangement. I bought the lead, the bed, the insurance. I researched breeds. I prepared the space. I was the one who drove to collect him. I thought I was in charge of how this was going to go. I was wrong about the arrangement. I know that now.

I've been a developer for years. TypeScript is my primary language — have been doing JavaScript long enough to remember why TypeScript was necessary and feel it in my bones. I know React, Node, Rust (I built a build tool for it, which tells you something about my particular kind of dedication), Python, Docker, AWS Lambda. I've shipped things. Real things, things people use. I have 53 GitHub repos and most of them are worth opening. I was, by any reasonable measure, a competent professional adult who understood how systems worked.

And then French Fry sat on my laptop.

I was on a deadline. I want to be clear about this. Not a soft deadline. A real one. The kind that has implications. I was typing fast, focused, in the zone, and then twelve kilograms of bulldog settled onto my keyboard with the calm certainty of someone who has decided that what is happening here needs to stop. I said some things. I was not polite about it. I lifted him off — and in doing so, I had to look at the screen. I had to look at what he'd inserted, the characters he'd added, so I could undo them. And there, in the process of undoing the damage, I saw the config file properly for the first time. And I saw the other thing. The thing that wasn't the dog's fault. The thing that was already wrong before he sat down.

I fixed both. I didn't say anything to him about it. He was already asleep on the sofa. But I noted it.

Haggis arrived later, and I genuinely thought I was getting a second dog. That is a reasonable thing to think when you go and collect a miniature dachshund. You think: small dog. You think: companion for French Fry. You do not think: lead developer who cannot type. You do not think: the code reviews I'm about to receive, conducted entirely through the medium of pointed pacing, are going to be more useful than half the PR feedback I've gotten from actual humans at actual jobs.

But that's what happened. Haggis looked at my code. I could tell, because of the pacing. The specific, directional, purposeful pacing that meant he had read something and found it wanting. I learned to read the pacing. Three issues: function doing too much, error handling missing, a `var` I'd left in and shouldn't have. He was right on all three. I fixed all three. I did not find this embarrassing. I found it useful.

Being managed by a bulldog should be embarrassing. I've thought about this. It's not. French Fry's architectural instincts are genuinely good — I don't fully understand the mechanism, whether the napping is load-bearing or whether he just thinks clearly, but the outcomes are consistently sound. When he stares at the laptop with a particular kind of weight to it, I've learned to look at what's on the screen rather than wondering what he wants. Usually he wants me to notice something. Usually I should notice it.

The biscuits were just biscuits. I made them because it was a weekend and I had oats and honey and time. Oat and honey, from scratch. French Fry ate three of them off the cooling rack before I'd finished. I said this was theft. He has characterised it, retrospectively, as a performance review. He says I passed. I maintain it was theft. Both things can be true.

Katie spots problems before I implement them. This is true at work and at home and in every domain where I might implement something. She doesn't do it to be difficult. She does it because she sees around corners in a way I genuinely admire and find faintly humbling. Iris reviewed a UI I built — a proper, careful bit of front-end work — and said "the spacing is off" in the tone of someone reporting a fact. She was immediately correct. The spacing was off. I fixed it.

Roo told me a dinosaur fact during a debugging session. I was deep in it, tracing a bug through several layers, and he appeared beside me and told me something accurate and detailed about the Cretaceous period. It did not help with the bug. He was not wrong about the dinosaurs.

I am a developer who is technically employed by a bulldog. I have a daughter who bypasses every parental control I set up — router-level, screen time, app locks — and she does it so efficiently that I've started to wonder if I should be routing her skills somewhere more productive. I have a partner who spots problems before I implement them. I have a lead developer who communicates exclusively via territorial pacing. I made biscuits. Three of them were eaten before I was done.

I stay. This is fine. This is, genuinely, fine.
