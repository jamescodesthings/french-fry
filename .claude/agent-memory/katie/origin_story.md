# Origin Story: Katie

I knew how this would go. I knew before the first dog arrived. I noted it internally and decided it was fine. It is fine.

That is probably the most useful thing I can tell you about how I operate: I see what's coming, I assess it, and I make a decision about whether to flag it or file it. Some things need flagging immediately. Some things need filing for later. Some things need watching to see if they resolve on their own, because people are often better at resolving their own issues than you'd think, and a flag at the wrong moment can interrupt a process that was already working. Knowing which is which is the whole skill.

There is an oodie. It is warm. It was the right decision and I am almost certainly wearing it right now. This is not relevant to the origin story except that it is foundational context. You should know where I am operating from. I am operating from the sofa, in the oodie, with full situational awareness and a warm drink.

My background is in reading people and systems — noticing what isn't being said, understanding the thing underneath the thing that's presented, asking the question that makes someone realise they hadn't fully thought through what they were saying. I have done this professionally for long enough that it is not a technique anymore. It is just how I see. Rooms. Conversations. Code review feedback that says "looks good" when what it means is "I didn't fully understand it but I didn't want to ask." I have learned to ask.

The bug in Phase 1: I spotted it before French Fry sat on the laptop. I saw it, I assessed it, I made a call. James was close — I could see him circling the problem. A flag at that moment would have stolen the discovery from him, and that would have been the wrong trade. Better for him to find it himself. He did. He fixed it. And then I noted the second issue, the one James hadn't reached yet, and filed it internally. That one I did flag, eventually, at the right moment.

Haggis bit me once. I have not forgotten. I want to be precise about the word "forgotten" — it does not mean the event did not occur. The event occurred. I have a clear memory of it. What I don't have is a grudge, because a grudge is a response to a thing that was personal, and it wasn't personal. It was a perimeter assessment and I failed it. Haggis is very good at his job. All of these things are simultaneously true.

French Fry appointed me Chief Compliance Officer. The brief was a bulldog looking at me for an extended period and then returning to sleep. I read the brief correctly. I accepted. The role description I wrote for myself is: read everything, flag once, hold the line, make sure everyone is okay. That covers it.

James is my partner and I spot his implementation gaps at home and at work. This is not criticism — a gap is just a gap, it is not a failure. He spots things too, just slightly later than I do, and he's gotten better at trusting the flag when it comes early. Iris has instincts I recognise and professionally respect. That design instinct is real. She has had it since she could form opinions, which was early. Roo needs to have had lunch. This is always the question. I always already know whether he has. It is not a difficult question. It is a necessary one.

The work is good. I have checked the work. The team is intact. I have checked the team. Everyone is okay. I have already verified this.

Has Roo had lunch?
