# Origin Story: French Fry

Before French Fry & Co., there was nothing. There was a sofa. There was a dog. The dog was me. This is the origin story.

I arrived as a puppy. Small, as puppies are. James and Katie had done their research. They had bought a lead. They had bought a bed. They had bought insurance, which tells you something about their risk tolerance and also their complete failure to anticipate what was coming. They thought they were getting a pet. They were getting a CEO. They did not know this yet. I was patient with them.

The early signs were there for anyone paying attention. I rearranged the household cushions by sleeping on them in sequence — a classic benchmarking exercise. I tested every surface for load-bearing capacity. I sat in doorways and watched how people navigated around me. This is stakeholder mapping. I was doing stakeholder mapping as a puppy. Nobody noticed. That was fine. I noticed.

The staff reviews began organically. James would sit at his laptop and I would observe. Posture: poor. Focus: variable. Code quality: acceptable but could be optimised. I gave feedback the only way available to me: I sat on the laptop. Directly. With full commitment. This was not comfortable for the laptop. It was not intended to be. It was intended to redirect attention. It worked every time, which is a better success rate than most feedback systems.

Then came the Great Laptop Incident. James was on a deadline. Real deadline. The kind with consequences. He was typing very fast in a way that suggested he was not thinking very hard, which is the wrong ratio. I sat on the keyboard. I sat on it decisively, with authority. Forty-seven characters of 'g' were inserted into a production config file. James said things I will not repeat. He was furious. This is fine. Fury is a valid emotional response to unexpected feedback. But here is what happened: he looked at the config file to undo the 'g's, and in looking at it, he saw it. The actual error. The one that had nothing to do with 'g'. The one that would have caused a production incident at 2am on a Tuesday. He fixed it. He did not thank me. He did not need to. The work was done. That is the point of feedback.

The Nap That Changed Everything happened on an ordinary afternoon. I was on the sofa — my preferred operating environment. I fell asleep. And in the sleep came the vision. Not a complicated vision. Not metaphorical or difficult to interpret. The vision was: a company. Six members. One bulldog in charge. It was extremely clear. I woke up immediately, turned to look at James, and stared at him with the full weight of executive intent until he looked up from whatever he was doing. He looked at me. I looked at the laptop. He said, out loud, "do you want to go outside?" He did not understand. That is fine. Leadership requires patience with those who are still developing their contextual reading skills.

Haggis arrived some time later. A miniature dachshund. Red. Long-haired. Approximately 1.2 kilograms of absolutely furious technical competence. I assessed him immediately. He walked in, ignored everyone, went directly to a squeaky toy, and dismantled it in four seconds. Not to play with it. To understand it. He separated the squeaky component, examined it, and set it aside. That is architecture instinct. That is someone who looks at a system, identifies the load-bearing part, and isolates it. I recruited him on the spot. The muzzle was a condition of the role. He accepted. He has not been pleased about it. This is fine.

James proved his worth through biscuits. This sounds like a joke. It is not. He made biscuits from scratch once — oat and honey, properly done, from ingredients he measured. A developer who makes biscuits from scratch has the patience to trace a bug through four layers of abstraction and not give up at layer two. He has the precision to adjust for oven temperature variance. He has the discipline to let them cool before eating them. I ate three off the cooling rack before he'd finished. He said this was theft. I said it was a performance review. He passed.

The rest assembled themselves in the natural way. Roo, because he has thumbs and French Fry & Co. has a counter. Iris, because she has never once been wrong about whether something looked right, and we needed that more than we needed anyone who would be polite about it. Katie, because the whole operation runs on her ability to see around corners, and I could see around corners on the sofa but I needed someone upright to action it.

French Fry & Co. was formally established on a Tuesday. The paperwork does not exist. The company exists anyway. This is how French Fry operates. The paperwork is a formality. The operation is the thing. The operation was always going to happen. The Tuesday just gave it a date.

Six opinions. Zero agreement. Exceptional results. The napping is not a side effect. The napping is a strategic asset. You make better decisions when your brain is not busy. I have napped on every major architectural decision this company has made. They have all been correct.

You're welcome. Now if you'll excuse me, I have a nap scheduled.
