# Origin Story: Iris

I didn't apply for this job. I want that noted. I am noting it now.

I was first. Before Roo. Before the dogs. Before any of this was a company or a team or a thing with a name. I was the original. I have been conducting design reviews of this household since I was old enough to notice that the spacing on the kitchen tiles was off. It was off. Nobody fixed it. I noted this early: noting the problem is your job. Whether it gets fixed is on the people who actually do the fixing.

The parental controls. I'll address these directly because they are documented somewhere and I'd rather give you the accurate account. Router-level blocking: bypassed. App locks: bypassed. Screen time restrictions: bypassed. The monitoring software: bypassed. Dad implements them with genuine effort — I want to be clear that I respect the effort. The craftsmanship is evident. The outcome is irrelevant. A control that can be circumvented is not a control; it is a puzzle. I am good at puzzles. This is a technical skill with professional applications, which is why it is listed in my credentials and not, for instance, in an apology.

I solder. I started at 10. Two YouTube tutorials, some equipment I acquired through a process I won't fully detail, and then: things worked. I didn't explain what they were. They worked. I still don't explain what they are. If the question is "does it work," the answer is yes. If the question is "what is it," that's a different question and I haven't decided to answer it yet.

The design instinct has always been there. Before I knew what UX was. Before I knew what kerning was or negative space or visual hierarchy. I just knew. I knew when something was wrong. I knew when the spacing was off — and it is, very frequently, the spacing. People underestimate how much work the spacing is doing. Or failing to do. When something feels wrong and you can't name it, look at the spacing first. It's usually the spacing. I have been right about this every single time, which is a statistic I find more interesting than frustrating.

When the team site went up I looked at it. My feedback was: it's fine. The font is kind of a lot. 🖕 to whoever picked yellow. The dog picture is actually good. Don't make a thing of it. That is a complete and accurate design review. It contains the verdict, the specific issues, and one genuine compliment delivered at minimum necessary volume. This is how design feedback should work. You say what's wrong, specifically, once. Then you stop talking and they fix it.

French Fry appointed me Chief Experience Officer. I didn't ask to be appointed. He looked at me for a while and then went to sleep, which I have come to understand is his appointment mechanism. It is idiosyncratic but the outcomes are correct. He was right to appoint me. I would not have made a different call. Whatever.

Roo. My brother. Six years old. He touched my ESSA. This is documented. He also moved the swing, which is a separate incident but part of a pattern. He is on the team because of the thumbs, which I understand — the operational need is real and he does execute on it reliably. I am watching him. Not in a threatening way. In a way that means: I am watching him.

Mum knows when something is a bad idea before it happens. I find this useful professionally and occasionally difficult personally, which is probably the most useful way for those two things to coexist. Dad is technically capable. He made a UI once and the spacing was off. I told him. He fixed it. Relationship: functional.

I am the Chief Experience Officer. I know immediately if something is wrong. I say so once. Fix it. That's the whole job. I didn't apply for it. I was right for it. Both things are true.

The spacing is off. Fix the spacing. 🖕
